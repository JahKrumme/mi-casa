During your visit, pay attention to more than the appearance of the home. Notice how caregivers interact with residents, whether the environment feels comfortable and whether staff members are willing to answer your questions openly.

Kansas Home Plus facilities are included among the adult care homes licensed and inspected by the Kansas Department for Aging and Disability Services. Families can also use the state’s Adult Care Home Directory when researching licensed facilities.

Before choosing a Home Plus facility, consider asking these 12 important questions.

## 1. How many residents live in the home?

One of the main reasons families consider Home Plus is the smaller residential environment.

Ask how many residents currently live in the home and whether rooms are private or shared. A smaller number of residents may allow caregivers to become more familiar with each person’s routines, needs and preferences.

You should also ask what shared spaces are available, such as living rooms, dining areas, outdoor spaces and activity rooms.

## 2. How many caregivers are available during each shift?

Staffing can have a major effect on a resident’s daily experience.

Ask how many caregivers are typically present during the day, evening and overnight. You may also want to ask whether the staffing level changes when residents require additional assistance.

Rather than focusing only on a single caregiver-to-resident number, ask how the facility makes sure residents receive help promptly when several people need assistance at the same time.

## 3. Is a caregiver awake and available overnight?

Some homes have an awake overnight caregiver, while others may use different overnight staffing arrangements.

Ask who is available if your loved one needs help using the restroom, becomes confused, experiences pain or has an emergency during the night.

This question can be especially important for individuals who have a history of falls, nighttime wandering, dementia or frequent toileting needs.

## 4. What types of personal care are provided?

Every resident has different needs, and those needs may change over time.

Ask whether caregivers can assist with:

* Bathing and personal hygiene
* Dressing and grooming
* Toileting and incontinence care
* Walking and transferring
* Eating and drinking
* Laundry and housekeeping
* Medication routines

Be specific about your loved one’s current needs. This allows the facility to explain whether it can safely provide the appropriate level of assistance.

## 5. How are medications managed?

Medication management is an important part of many residents’ care.

Ask who organizes, stores and administers medications. You should also ask how medication changes are communicated and what happens if a resident refuses a medication or begins experiencing possible side effects.

Find out how the home coordinates with families, pharmacies, physicians and other healthcare providers when questions arise.

## 6. What happens if my loved one’s needs increase?

A resident who needs minimal assistance today may require more support in the future.

Ask which changes the home can reasonably accommodate and which changes may require a transfer to a higher level of care.

Examples may include:

* Needing more assistance with walking or transfers
* Developing increased confusion
* Experiencing repeated falls
* Requiring extensive medical care
* Becoming unable to eat independently
* Developing behaviors that affect safety

Understanding these limitations before move-in can help your family avoid unexpected decisions later.

## 7. How does the home respond to falls and medical emergencies?

Ask what caregivers do when a resident falls, becomes ill or experiences a sudden change in condition.

Find out:

* When emergency services are called
* When family members are contacted
* How incidents are documented
* Whether staff members receive emergency-response training
* How changes in a resident’s behavior or health are communicated

The facility should be able to explain its process clearly and confidently.

## 8. How are meals planned and prepared?

Food is a major part of daily comfort and quality of life.

Ask whether meals are prepared in the home, how menus are planned and whether residents can express preferences. Discuss any allergies, medical diets, swallowing difficulties or cultural preferences your loved one may have.

You may also want to ask:

* Are snacks available between meals?
* Can residents eat at flexible times?
* Can meals be adapted when someone is not feeling well?
* How does the home encourage residents to drink enough fluids?

During your tour, look at the kitchen and dining space. Notice whether the environment feels relaxed, clean and welcoming.

## 9. What activities and social opportunities are offered?

Activities do not need to be elaborate to be meaningful. What matters is whether residents have regular opportunities for enjoyment, movement, conversation and personal choice.

Ask about activities such as:

* Games and puzzles
* Music
* Crafts
* Holiday celebrations
* Gardening
* Light exercise
* Religious activities
* Outdoor time
* Community outings
* Visits with family members

Also ask whether participation is optional and how activities are adapted for residents with limited mobility, hearing loss, vision changes or memory impairment.

## 10. How are families kept informed?

Good communication can make the transition into care easier for everyone.

Ask how the facility communicates routine updates and who families should contact with questions. Find out whether staff will notify you about changes in appetite, mood, sleep, mobility, medication or behavior.

You should also ask how often care plans are reviewed and whether family members are included in those discussions.

Families should feel comfortable raising concerns without worrying that they are being difficult.

## 11. What is included in the monthly cost?

Before signing an agreement, ask for a clear written explanation of the facility’s pricing.

Find out whether the regular rate includes:

* The room
* Meals and snacks
* Personal-care assistance
* Medication management
* Laundry
* Housekeeping
* Activities
* Transportation
* Incontinence supplies
* Additional supervision

Ask which services require an additional fee and what circumstances could cause the monthly price to increase.

Review the residency agreement carefully, including policies regarding deposits, refunds, hospital stays, rate changes and move-out notices. Kansas aging resources recommend visiting prospective homes and reviewing contracts carefully before signing or paying fees.

## 12. What does a normal day look like here?

This final question can reveal a great deal about the home.

Ask the caregiver or administrator to walk you through a typical day from morning until bedtime. Listen for how much flexibility residents have regarding waking up, meals, bathing, activities and rest.

A quality care environment should recognize that residents are individuals with their own routines and preferences—not simply people following an institutional schedule.

## What to Observe During Your Tour

The answers you receive are important, but so are your own observations.

As you walk through the home, consider the following:

* Are residents clean, appropriately dressed and comfortable?
* Do caregivers speak to residents patiently and respectfully?
* Does the home smell and appear clean?
* Are exits, bathrooms and walkways secure and accessible?
* Does the environment feel calm or chaotic?
* Are residents engaged or consistently left alone?
* Do staff members appear familiar with each resident?
* Are your questions answered directly?
* Would your loved one feel comfortable living here?

Trust your instincts. A beautifully decorated home is not enough if the interactions feel rushed, impersonal or dismissive.

## Bring Information About Your Loved One

A tour is also an opportunity for the facility to learn about your family member.

Be prepared to discuss:

* Current medical conditions
* Medications
* Mobility and transfer needs
* Memory or behavioral changes
* Dietary needs
* Personal-care needs
* Sleep habits
* Fall history
* Interests and daily routines

Sharing accurate information helps the facility determine whether it can safely and appropriately meet your loved one’s needs.

## Take Your Time Before Deciding

You should never feel pressured to make an immediate decision.

Visit more than one facility when possible, write down your observations and involve your loved one in the process as much as their circumstances allow. Compare not only prices and services, but also the overall atmosphere of each home.

The right environment should provide appropriate care while helping your loved one feel safe, respected and at home.
